Unfare Enforcement: How Inequality Plagues Subway Enforcement

As tensions build between transit officers and activists, data reveals a painful truth about fare enforcement

By: Parker E. Quinlan

Protestors in Brooklyn gathered near Hoyt-Schermerhorn Street Station after viral video showing a teenager being arrested by transit police with their guns drawn. (Photo By Parker E. Quinlan)

Nearly one week after protests broke out in downtown Brooklyn over transit enforcement, activists are asking whether the police are unfairly targeting minorities in the campaign to target turnstile jumpers.

Though activists claim that police unfairly target minorities for fare enforcement, data at the transit district-level suggests location, more than race alone, determines who is being ticketed.

Transit District 11, for example, which covers the eastern half of the Bronx, sees a nearly 93% rate of black and Hispanic individuals being ticketed for violating turnstiles.

Contrast this against Transit District 01, which covers the Upper West Side and much of Midtown Manhattan, which has a much more heterogeneous group of fare violators being ticketed, with nearly 26% being white.

Though in every transit district black and Hispanic violators make up a majority of all tickets written, data reveals some districts more profoundly target these populations than others.
